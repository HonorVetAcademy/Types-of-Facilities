The Big Picture — How US Healthcare Is Organized

Before you place a single candidate, you need to understand the three main pillars of US healthcare. Think of them as three different "worlds" — each with different patients, different staff, and different staffing needs.

🏥Acute CareRegular hospitals — where patients go for emergencies, surgery, and short-term illness
⚕️LTACHSpecialized hospitals for very sick patients who need longer care after leaving a regular hospital
🏡LTCNursing homes — where patients live long-term when they can no longer care for themselves
🚑TraumaSpecial levels (I, II, III) inside acute care hospitals that handle accident and injury victims
🎖️VAGovernment hospitals serving military veterans — HonorVet's most important client type
📖 REAL EXAMPLE — HOW ONE PATIENT MOVES THROUGH THE SYSTEM

One Patient. Four Facilities. Your Job Openings at Every Step.

Here's why understanding facility types matters. When a patient gets seriously injured or ill, they don't just go to one place — they move through the healthcare system. Each stop needs staff. That staff needs to come from somewhere. That's you.

Step 1 — Acute Care Hospital: Patient arrives at the emergency room. Nurses, doctors, and techs stabilize them. They might spend a week in the ICU after surgery. Your placements: ER Nurse, ICU Nurse, OR Tech.

Step 2 — LTACH: Patient improves but still needs daily medical treatment and a ventilator to breathe. They're too complex for a nursing home but don't need a full hospital. They go to an LTACH for a few weeks. Your placements: ICU-trained Nurse, Respiratory Therapist.

Step 3 — Rehab Hospital (IRF): Patient is off the ventilator and needs physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy daily to regain function. Your placements: PT, OT, Speech Therapist.

Step 4 — LTC / Nursing Home: Patient is stable but needs 24-hour nursing care and help with daily activities (eating, dressing, bathing). They may stay here long-term. Your placements: RN, LPN, CNA.

🏆 Key takeaway: One patient's journey creates staffing needs across 4 different facility types. When you build relationships with hospitals, ask if they have a rehab center, nursing home, or LTACH in their network — that's 4x the placement opportunities.

Acute Care Hospitals

The most common type of healthcare facility — and where most of your placements will happen. Think of acute care as the "main hospital" that most people picture when they hear the word "hospital."

🏥 WHAT IS AN ACUTE CARE HOSPITAL?

In Simple Terms

An acute care hospital is where people go when they have a medical emergency, need surgery, or have a serious illness that needs immediate treatment. The goal is always the same: treat the patient and send them home (or to the next level of care) as quickly as safely possible.

Think of it in three phases: the patient arrives through the Emergency Department (ER), gets treated and possibly admitted to a hospital floor or the ICU (Intensive Care Unit — for the sickest patients), then when they're stable enough, they either go home or move to a rehab center, nursing home, or LTACH.

💡 Average stay: Most patients stay 3–5 days in an acute care hospital. The hospital gets paid based on the patient's diagnosis — so the goal is always to treat efficiently and discharge safely.

Hospitals aren't one big room — they're divided into specialized units. As a recruiter, you'll hear these unit names constantly. Here's what each one means:

  • Emergency Department (ED / ER): Where patients arrive for urgent care. Nurses here see everything — from broken bones to heart attacks. Very fast-paced. Needs nurses who can multitask and stay calm under pressure.
  • ICU (Intensive Care Unit): The unit for the sickest patients who need one-on-one or one-on-two nursing care. Patients here are often on breathing machines (ventilators) or heart monitors. ICU nurses are the highest-paid nurses in hospitals.
  • Step-Down / Telemetry (Tele): A "middle ground" between ICU and a regular floor. Patients here are improving but still need heart monitoring. Think of it as the ICU's waiting room — patients come here before going to a regular floor.
  • Medical-Surgical (Med-Surg): The most common hospital unit. General medical patients — someone recovering from surgery, a patient with pneumonia, a diabetic patient getting their blood sugar controlled. This is the highest-volume placement area for recruiters.
  • Operating Room (OR): Where surgeries happen. Needs surgical nurses (called Circulating RNs) and Surgical Techs (CSTs) who hand instruments to surgeons.
  • Labor & Delivery (L&D): Where babies are born. Specialized nurses who support mothers during childbirth. Separate from regular nursing — requires specific training and certifications.
✅ New recruiter tip: When a hospital sends you a job order, always ask "What unit is this for?" — ICU, Med-Surg, ER, Tele, OR, and L&D are all very different jobs, even if the title says "Registered Nurse."

Here are the clinical roles you'll be placing most often in acute care hospitals:

  • Registered Nurse (RN): The core of hospital staffing. RNs assess patients, give medications, coordinate care, and communicate with doctors. Must have a nursing license. Most travel contracts are for RNs.
  • Licensed Practical Nurse (LPN / LVN): A step below RN — does many of the same hands-on tasks but with more limited scope. Less common in hospitals, more common in nursing homes and clinics.
  • Certified Nursing Assistant (CNA): Helps patients with basic needs — bathing, feeding, turning in bed, taking vital signs. Works under RN supervision. Common in hospitals and nursing homes.
  • Respiratory Therapist (RT / RRT): Specialist who manages patients' breathing — ventilators, oxygen therapy, breathing treatments. In high demand at hospitals with ICUs and LTACHs.
  • Surgical Tech (CST / ST): Assists in operating rooms — sets up surgical equipment, hands instruments to surgeons. Needs OR experience.
  • Hospitalist (MD/DO): A doctor whose entire job is managing admitted hospital patients. Common locum tenens (temporary doctor) placement role.
📋 What to ask every nursing candidate: "What unit do you work on? ICU, Med-Surg, ER, Tele?" This one question tells you almost everything about where they can be placed.

As a recruiter, you'll talk about "bill rates" — what the hospital pays the agency per hour for each clinician. Here are typical ranges to know:

  • ICU Nurse: $90–$135/hr — highest-paid nursing role. The sicker the patient, the higher the rate.
  • ER Nurse: $75–$110/hr — fast-paced, high-skill, well paid.
  • Med-Surg Nurse: $55–$85/hr — the most common placement, steady volume.
  • Surgical Tech: $45–$70/hr — specialist role, good demand.
  • Respiratory Therapist: $55–$85/hr — in shortage nationally, especially for ICU and LTACH.
⚠️ Remember: The bill rate is what the hospital pays the agency. The candidate's take-home pay is different — it's a portion of the bill rate. Never quote the bill rate to a candidate; quote their pay package.

Long-Term Acute Care Hospital (LTACH)

Think of an LTACH as a hospital for patients who are too sick for a nursing home but don't need a full emergency hospital anymore. They need specialized medical care — just slower and for a longer period.

⚕️ WHAT IS AN LTACH? IN PLAIN ENGLISH

The "In-Between" Hospital

Imagine a patient who had a major surgery, is recovering, but still needs a breathing machine (ventilator) to help them breathe. They don't need emergency surgery anymore — but they're not ready for a nursing home either. They go to an LTACH.

The key rule: patients at an LTACH must stay an average of 25 days or more. This is what makes it different from a regular hospital (where the average stay is 3–5 days). LTACHs partner with regular hospitals — when a hospital patient is stable enough to leave but still needs complex care, they transfer to the LTACH. This frees up the hospital's beds for new patients.

Common conditions treated at LTACHs: Patients on breathing machines (ventilators), serious wounds that need daily dressing changes, severe infections, and patients who need multiple IV medications and 24/7 medical supervision.

💡 Recruiter tip: LTACHs need nurses with ICU experience — not brand-new nurses. The patients are complex, but the pace is slower than a trauma ICU. This makes LTACHs a great option for experienced ICU nurses who want less chaos but still challenging work.

🏥 Acute Care Hospital

SHORT-TERM STAY

  • Average stay: 4–5 days
  • Many nurses needed per shift

⚕️ LTACH

LONG-TERM STAY
  • Treats complex patients post-hospital
  • Goal: manage complex conditions slowly

  • Critical Care / ICU Nurse (RN): The main nursing role. Must have experience working with very sick patients. Typically needs 1–2 years of ICU or step-down experience. Bill rate: $65–$90/hr.
  • Respiratory Therapist (RT/RRT): Manages breathing machines and helps patients slowly breathe on their own again. This is the most in-demand and hardest-to-fill role at LTACHs nationally.
  • Wound Care Nurse (WOCN): Specialist in treating serious wounds — pressure injuries, surgical wounds, diabetic wounds. In shortage at most LTACHs.
  • Physical Therapist (PT) / Occupational Therapist (OT): Help patients regain strength and function while they're still medically fragile.
  • Hospitalist Doctor: A physician who oversees all the patients daily. Often a locum tenens (temporary doctor) placement.
✅ Pitch to burned-out ICU nurses: "Same clinical complexity, but slower pace. You'll actually get to know your patients over weeks, not hours. Less chaos, same challenge." Many experienced travel nurses love LTACHs once they try them.
  • Medicare pays LTACHs a little more in 2026 (+3%). This means facilities are financially stable and actively hiring — good news for your placements.
  • Watch for LTACH closures in your market. When an LTACH closes, the patients have to go somewhere — usually creating urgent staffing needs at the partner acute care hospital. If you hear about a closure, call the local hospitals immediately.
  • LTACHs are expanding their Social Work teams. Starting 2026, they need to document patients' social needs (housing, food, transportation) at admission. If you place Social Workers or Case Managers, LTACHs are a growing market.
📌 Bottom line for a new recruiter: LTACH is a stable, growing placement market. Respiratory Therapists and Wound Care Nurses are your highest-demand roles here.

Long-Term Care (LTC) — Nursing Homes & Skilled Nursing

Long-Term Care is where patients live when they can no longer take care of themselves at home and don't need a hospital. Think of it as "a home with nurses" — 24-hour care for people with ongoing health needs.

2026 MAJOR CHANGE — LTC STAFFING RULE. HERE'S WHAT IT MEANS FOR YOU.
🏡 WHAT IS LONG-TERM CARE? IN PLAIN ENGLISH

Not a Hospital. Not a Hotel. Something In Between.

LTC facilities (also called nursing homes, skilled nursing facilities, or SNFs) provide round-the-clock care for people who cannot safely live at home. This includes elderly people with dementia, stroke survivors who need ongoing therapy, people with Parkinson's disease, or anyone whose condition requires daily nursing supervision.

The key difference from a hospital: the goal isn't to "fix" the patient and send them home. Many LTC residents stay for months or years — this is their home. Staff help with daily activities of living (ADLs) — bathing, dressing, eating, using the bathroom, getting around. This is called "custodial care."

💡 Easy way to remember: Hospital = short-term, fix the problem. LTACH = medium-term, manage complex medical needs. LTC = long-term, help with daily life.
🧓

Who Are the Patients?

Mostly older adults (65+), though LTC serves all ages. Common patients: someone with advanced dementia who can't live alone, a stroke survivor who needs ongoing care, a person with severe COPD who needs daily oxygen and nursing supervision.

Long-Term Residents
🏠

Types of LTC Facilities

Nursing Homes: Traditional custodial care, mostly government-funded. Skilled Nursing Facilities (SNFs): Higher level — also do physical/occupational therapy for Medicare patients. Assisted Living: Less medical oversight, more residential. Memory Care: Specialized units for dementia patients.

Multiple Models
⚕️

Who Works in LTC?

Very different from a hospital. CNAs do most of the hands-on daily care. LPNs give medications and do clinical tasks. RNs manage the clinical oversight. PT/OT/SLP do rehabilitation. The ratio is much more CNA/LPN heavy than hospitals.

Different Staff Mix
🔔 This is the biggest change in LTC in years. Know the basics before your first LTC client call.

What changed: In 2024, a new law required nursing homes to have a Registered Nurse (RN) present 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. In December 2025, that law was repealed (cancelled). The rule now is: nursing homes need an RN on-site for at least 8 hours per day.

What this means for recruiters:

  • Some nursing homes may try to reduce nursing staff — but the best facilities will keep strong staffing because it affects their quality ratings and reputation
  • The nursing shortage in LTC did NOT go away — facilities still struggle to find enough CNAs, LPNs, and RNs regardless of what the law says
  • State laws vary — some states (like California and New York) have their own, stricter staffing rules. Always check the state before making promises to a client about staffing requirements
⚠️ What to tell candidates who ask: "The federal minimum changed, but quality facilities still staff well above the minimum. Your job is safe — the demand for LTC nurses is not going away."
  • CNA (Certified Nursing Assistant): The most common LTC role and the hardest to fill. CNAs do the heavy lifting — literally. They help patients bathe, dress, eat, and move. Travel bill rate: ~$28–$42/hr. Extremely high turnover — always in demand.
  • LPN (Licensed Practical Nurse): Gives medications, does basic clinical assessments, supervises CNAs. Much more responsibility in LTC than in hospitals. Bill rate: $40–$65/hr.
  • RN (Registered Nurse): Manages the clinical picture — assessments, care plan updates, physician communication. Bill rate: $60–$85/hr. The Director of Nursing (DON) at a facility must be an RN.
  • PT / OT / SLP: Physical, Occupational, and Speech Therapists. Essential for Medicare-funded rehab stays (up to 100 days). Bill rate: $55–$75/hr.
✅ LTC is a volume game. Individual bill rates are lower than hospitals, but facilities always need staff and they need a lot of it. One nursing home system with 10 facilities can be a massive, reliable client.

Every nursing home in the US gets a public star rating (1–5 stars) from the government. Families use this to choose where to place their loved ones. As a recruiter, you should check this before working with a new LTC client.

  • 5-star facilities: Well-run, good reputation. Likely to maintain strong staffing and be good long-term clients. Harder to get contracts with, but worth it.
  • 3–4 star facilities: Average. Good volume clients, reasonable to work with.
  • 1–2 star facilities: May have compliance problems, poor management, or financial instability. Contracts may be short or unpaid. Approach with caution.
🔍 How to check: Go to Medicare's "Care Compare" website and search any nursing home by name. The star rating is publicly available. Make this a habit before signing a new LTC client.

Other Facility Types You'll Encounter

Beyond hospitals, LTACHs, and nursing homes, there are several other facility types you'll see in job orders. Here's a quick introduction to each.

An Academic Medical Center (AMC) is a hospital connected to a medical school — like Mayo Clinic, Johns Hopkins, or Cleveland Clinic. They treat the most complex cases in the country and also train doctors, nurses, and other healthcare workers.

A Teaching Hospital is any hospital where medical students and residents (doctors-in-training) are learning. They're affiliated with medical schools but may not have the same research focus as a full AMC.

  • What makes them different: More complex patients, higher credentialing requirements, longer onboarding process. Not ideal for newer travel nurses.
  • What they pay: Premium rates — experienced specialty nurses can command $80–$120/hr.
  • Recruiter tip: July is a surge month — new resident doctors start in July every year, creating a temporary gap in clinical coverage that hospitals fill with experienced travel staff.

A Magnet Hospital is a hospital that has earned a special award (from an organization called ANCC) for having excellent nursing standards. Only about 10% of US hospitals have Magnet status — roughly 600 facilities.

  • What it means for you: These hospitals often require nurses to have a Bachelor's degree in nursing (BSN). Always check if your candidate has a BSN before submitting to a Magnet hospital.
  • Why candidates care: A Magnet placement looks great on a nurse's resume — it shows they worked at an elite nursing environment. Use this as a selling point when recruiting.
  • Pay: $80–$110/hr for specialty nurses.

A Critical Access Hospital is a small hospital in a rural area — often the only hospital for 35+ miles. They can have no more than 25 beds and are specially funded by Medicare to stay open, because if they closed, the community would have no healthcare access.

  • There are ~1,700 CAHs in the US — mostly in the Midwest, South, and Mountain West.
  • The nursing shortage here is severe — over 40% vacancy rates are common. These facilities desperately need staff.
  • Recruiter advantage: Less competition from other agencies. These clients are loyal and grateful when you show up for them.
  • Key pitch to candidates: Loan forgiveness programs — nurses who work in rural areas can get up to $50,000 of student loans forgiven. This is a powerful recruiting tool.
  • What they need most: Generalist nurses who can work multiple floors, Family Medicine doctors, and Nurse Practitioners.

The US Department of Veterans Affairs operates 170 hospitals (called VAMCs) across the country serving over 9 million military veterans. This is HonorVet's most strategic market — our company was founded to serve veterans, and our business certification (SDVOSB) gives us a competitive advantage in VA contracts.

  • What makes VA hospitals different: The patients are veterans. Clinicians need to understand military culture and veteran-specific health issues like PTSD, traumatic brain injury, and injuries related to combat or military service.
  • What they need most: Mental health nurses and psychiatrists, primary care doctors and nurse practitioners, and specialists in brain injury and spinal cord injury.
  • Our advantage: HonorVet is certified as a Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business (SDVOSB). The VA gives preference to companies like ours when awarding contracts. This is a real competitive edge — use it in every VA client conversation.
  • EMR note: VA hospitals use Oracle Health (Cerner). Ask your candidates if they have Cerner experience.
🎖️ HonorVet mission: We prioritize placing veteran clinicians at VA facilities whenever possible. If you find a veteran nurse or doctor who wants to work with fellow veterans — this is your placement.

Inpatient Rehabilitation Facility (IRF) — "Rehab Hospital": Where patients go to regain function after a major event (stroke, brain injury, hip fracture). They receive at least 3 hours of physical, occupational, or speech therapy every day. About 1,200 in the US. Main staffing needs: Physical Therapists, Occupational Therapists, Speech Therapists, and Rehab Nurses. Growing fast as the US population ages.

Federally Qualified Health Center (FQHC) — "Community Clinic": Government-funded clinics in underserved neighborhoods and rural areas that serve patients regardless of their ability to pay. Think of them as the safety-net primary care for low-income communities. Main staffing needs: Nurse Practitioners, Physician Assistants, Family Medicine doctors, and mental health counselors. Many offer student loan repayment to attract providers.

EMR Systems — The Computer Systems Nurses Use

Every hospital runs on a computer system where nurses document patient care. As a new recruiter, you need to know which system each facility type uses — and ask every candidate which ones they know. It takes 30 seconds and prevents a lot of wasted submissions.

💻 WHY EMR MATTERS TO YOU AS A RECRUITER

In Plain English: It's the Software Nurses Use Every Shift

An EMR (Electronic Medical Record) is the software that healthcare workers use to document everything about their patients — medicines given, vital signs, doctor's orders, nurse's notes. Every hospital has one. Every clinical worker uses it every single shift.

Here's why it matters to you: If a nurse learned on Epic and the hospital uses Cerner, they'll need extra training before they're fully productive. Hospitals know this and will sometimes decline a candidate who doesn't match their system — even if that candidate is otherwise perfect. One question prevents that rejection.

💡 The one rule to remember: Ask every candidate: "Which computer system (EMR) have you used at your last job, and when?" Then check what the facility uses. Simple. Fast. Effective.
⚡

The most widely used hospital software in the US. If your candidate works at a large hospital, there's a good chance they're on Epic. Used at big health systems, university hospitals, and Magnet-designated facilities.

The second-biggest hospital system and — most importantly for HonorVet — the system the VA is switching to across all 170 VA hospitals. Community hospitals use it too. If you're placing anyone at a VA, ask about Cerner.

🎖️ HonorVet priority: Candidates with Cerner experience are your most valuable candidates for VA placements. Always ask.
🟢
STANDARD FOR RURAL HOSPITALS

MEDITECH

The most common system at Critical Access Hospitals and small-to-mid community hospitals — especially in rural areas and the Northeast. If your candidate has worked at rural or smaller hospitals, they probably know MEDITECH.

Critical Access HospitalsSmall Community Hospitals
🌾 Rural placements: MEDITECH experience is perfect for CAH and rural contracts. These candidates are often undervalued — and they fill your hardest-to-place jobs.
🏡
LTC / NURSING HOME STANDARD

PointClickCare

This is the most important one for LTC placements. PointClickCare is the dominant software in nursing homes and skilled nursing facilities nationwide. It is completely different from hospital EMRs. A hospital nurse switching to LTC must learn this system.

LTC / Nursing Homes ✅Skilled Nursing Facilities
⚠️ Key mistake to avoid: Never assume a nurse with Epic experience can just step into an LTC role. PointClickCare is completely different — ask about it separately.
🌐
CLINICS & COMMUNITY HEALTH

eClinicalWorks & athenahealth

These are outpatient clinic systems — used at community health centers (FQHCs), primary care offices, and urgent care. Very different from hospital systems. If you're placing a Nurse Practitioner or Family Medicine doctor at a clinic, ask about these.

FQHCs / Community ClinicsPrimary Care Offices
💡 Note: These are outpatient-only systems. A candidate who only knows eCW or athena has NOT worked in a hospital. They're clinic-trained providers.
☀️
OTHERS YOU MAY ENCOUNTER

Allscripts / Sunrise / McKesson

Some smaller community hospitals use older or less-common systems like Allscripts Sunrise or McKesson Paragon. These facilities are often in the middle of switching to Epic or Cerner. If you encounter these, it's a sign the hospital may be in transition — which often creates urgent staffing needs.

Smaller Community HospitalsOften Mid-Migration
📋 Tip: If a client tells you they're switching EMR systems, that's an opportunity. System transitions create urgent, short-term staffing needs for extra experienced hands during changeover.

📞 YOUR ACTION PLAN — 3 SIMPLE QUESTIONS TO ASK EVERY CANDIDATE

That's All It Takes

1️⃣

"Which computer system do you use at work?"

Get the name — Epic, Cerner, MEDITECH, PointClickCare, or something else. Write it down. Include it in every submission note.

Always Ask
2️⃣

"When did you last use it?"

Experience from 3 years ago may not count for a facility that needs someone who can hit the ground running. Most facilities want recent, active experience.

Verify Recency
3️⃣

"How comfortable are you — 1 to 10?"

A quick self-rating tells you if they're a power user or just have basic exposure. A 7+ in the right system is a selling point you should mention in your submission email.

Use It to Sell

"Two nurses apply for the same ICU contract. One says 'Epic proficient, used daily for 18 months.' The other says 'EMR experience.' The hospital picks the first one — every single time. Be specific."
